Semiconductors:
The Silicon Foundation of the AI Revolution

David Kingsley, PhD
Scientist · Neural NeXus
March 2026 · 11 min read

Every breakthrough in artificial intelligence — every large language model, every image generator, every autonomous agent — runs on semiconductors. The algorithms get the headlines, but it is the chips that set the ceiling. Without continued advances in semiconductor design and manufacturing, the AI revolution stalls. Understanding the chip industry is not optional for anyone trying to understand where AI is going.

The semiconductor industry is a $600 billion global market, but its influence extends far beyond its revenue. Chips are the enabling substrate for virtually every technology sector: AI, cloud computing, autonomous vehicles, smartphones, defense systems, and medical devices. The companies that design and fabricate the most advanced chips hold extraordinary leverage over the trajectory of technology itself.

This guide covers what you need to know: how the chip industry is structured, which companies matter most, why AI has created unprecedented demand for specific types of semiconductors, and why chip manufacturing has become a geopolitical flashpoint.

Why Semiconductors Matter More Than Ever

The AI compute curve is staggering. Training a frontier language model in 2020 required roughly 3,000 GPU-hours. By 2024, training runs for models like GPT-4 and Gemini Ultra consumed tens of thousands of GPUs running for months, at costs exceeding $100 million per training run. By 2026, the largest training clusters contain over 100,000 GPUs, and the planned next-generation clusters aim for a million.

This exponential growth in compute demand has made semiconductors — specifically, AI accelerator chips — the most strategically important technology on Earth. The companies that can design and manufacture these chips are not just supplying a market; they are determining which organizations, and which countries, can participate in the AI race at all.

Moore's Law, the observation that transistor density on chips roughly doubles every two years, drove five decades of predictable performance gains. While the cadence has slowed at the physical level, innovations in chip architecture, packaging, and specialization have kept effective performance scaling alive — especially for AI workloads, where purpose-built accelerators deliver orders-of-magnitude improvements over general-purpose processors.

Chip Design vs. Fabrication: The Fabless Model

The modern semiconductor industry is built on a critical division of labor. Chip designers — companies like NVIDIA, AMD, Apple, and Qualcomm — create the architectures and circuit layouts for chips. Chip fabricators (foundries) — most importantly TSMC — actually manufacture the physical silicon. This is the fabless model, and it has defined the industry for two decades.

Why the split? Building a state-of-the-art semiconductor fabrication facility (a "fab") costs $20–30 billion and takes 3–5 years. The extreme ultraviolet (EUV) lithography machines required for leading-edge nodes cost over $300 million each, and only one company in the world — ASML, based in the Netherlands — makes them. The capital requirements and technical complexity are so extreme that only a handful of companies can afford to operate at the frontier.

TSMC (Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company) fabricates roughly 90% of the world's most advanced chips. This concentration creates both extraordinary efficiency and extraordinary risk. TSMC's manufacturing expertise is unmatched, but the fact that nearly all advanced chip production depends on a single company located on an island 100 miles from mainland China is one of the most discussed geopolitical vulnerabilities in the world.

AI Accelerators: GPUs, TPUs, and Custom ASICs

Not all chips are created equal when it comes to AI. Traditional CPUs (central processing units) are designed for general-purpose sequential computation. AI workloads — particularly the massive matrix multiplications involved in training and running neural networks — require a fundamentally different approach: massive parallelism.

GPUs (Graphics Processing Units) were originally designed for rendering graphics, which involves performing the same mathematical operation on millions of pixels simultaneously. This parallel architecture turns out to be nearly ideal for neural network computation. NVIDIA recognized this early and spent over a decade building a software ecosystem (CUDA) that made its GPUs the default platform for AI research. That early investment now constitutes one of the deepest competitive moats in technology.

TPUs (Tensor Processing Units) are Google's custom-designed AI accelerators, optimized specifically for tensor operations used in machine learning. Google uses TPUs internally for training and inference across its products, and offers them to external customers through Google Cloud. TPUs are not general-purpose — they sacrifice flexibility for raw efficiency on AI workloads.

Custom ASICs (Application-Specific Integrated Circuits) are chips designed for a single specific task. Amazon's Trainium and Inferentia chips, Intel's Gaudi accelerators, and a growing number of startup designs (Cerebras, Groq, SambaNova) all represent bets that purpose-built silicon can outperform GPUs for specific AI workloads. The trade-off is always the same: specialization delivers efficiency, but at the cost of flexibility.

NVIDIA's dominance is not just hardware — it's ecosystem. CUDA, NVIDIA's proprietary software platform, has over 4 million developers and is deeply embedded in every major AI framework. Competitors don't just need to build better chips; they need to replicate a software ecosystem that took 15 years to build. This is why NVIDIA's data center GPU revenue grew from $15 billion in 2023 to over $100 billion in 2025 — and why the GPU shortage has constrained AI progress industry-wide.

The Companies That Matter

NVIDIA
GPU Design · USA

The undisputed leader in AI accelerators. The H100 and B200 GPUs power the majority of AI training worldwide. CUDA's software moat makes switching costs enormous. Market cap exceeded $3 trillion in 2025.

TSMC
Foundry · Taiwan

Fabricates ~90% of the world's most advanced chips. The sole manufacturer capable of producing at 3nm and below. Every major fabless chip designer depends on TSMC's fabs.

AMD
GPU + CPU Design · USA

NVIDIA's primary GPU competitor. The MI300X accelerator is gaining traction in AI data centers. AMD's open ROCm software stack is positioning as the open-source alternative to CUDA.

Intel
Design + Foundry · USA

The former semiconductor king, now fighting to regain relevance. Intel Foundry Services aims to become a TSMC alternative for Western chip production. Gaudi accelerators target enterprise AI workloads.

Qualcomm
Mobile + Edge AI · USA

Dominates mobile processors and is pushing aggressively into on-device AI. Snapdragon chips power the majority of Android devices and an increasing number of Windows PCs with integrated neural processing units.

ASML
Lithography Equipment · Netherlands

The sole manufacturer of EUV lithography machines required for leading-edge chip fabrication. Without ASML's equipment, no foundry can produce chips below 7nm. A true single point of failure for the industry.

Geopolitics: Chips as Strategic Assets

Semiconductors have become the most geopolitically contested technology since nuclear weapons. The concentration of advanced chip manufacturing in Taiwan, combined with the critical importance of chips for both AI and military applications, has made semiconductor supply chains a top national security priority for the United States, China, and the European Union.

The CHIPS and Science Act: Signed into law in 2022, this $52.7 billion U.S. initiative aims to rebuild domestic semiconductor manufacturing capacity. TSMC, Samsung, and Intel are all building new fabs on U.S. soil — TSMC's Arizona facility is the flagship project. But replicating the manufacturing ecosystem that Taiwan has built over decades will take years, and U.S.-made chips will initially be more expensive than those produced in Asia.

The Taiwan risk: Any disruption to TSMC's operations — whether from natural disaster, military conflict, or political instability — would cripple the global technology industry. There is no backup. TSMC's most advanced fabs are in Hsinchu and Tainan, and no other foundry can produce chips at comparable process nodes. This vulnerability is driving the reshoring push, but progress is slow and the cost is measured in hundreds of billions of dollars.

U.S.–China export controls: Since October 2022, the U.S. has imposed increasingly strict restrictions on the sale of advanced chips and chip-making equipment to China. These controls have cut Chinese AI labs off from the most powerful NVIDIA GPUs, forcing reliance on older architectures and domestic alternatives. China is investing heavily in semiconductor self-sufficiency, but remains at least 5–7 years behind TSMC at the leading edge.

Reshoring and allied supply chains: The broader strategic trend is toward "friendshoring" — building semiconductor capacity in allied nations. Japan, South Korea, Germany, and the U.S. are all investing in new fabs. The goal is not to replace TSMC but to reduce single-point-of-failure risk and ensure that critical chip production is not concentrated in a single geopolitical flashpoint.

What to Watch

The NVIDIA B200 and Rubin architecture: NVIDIA's next-generation AI accelerators promise another leap in training performance. Whether competitors can close the gap — or whether NVIDIA's lead continues to widen — will shape the AI compute landscape through the end of the decade.

TSMC Arizona ramp: The success or failure of TSMC's U.S. fab operations will determine whether semiconductor reshoring is viable at scale, or whether the cost and talent gaps prove insurmountable.

China's domestic chip progress: Huawei's Ascend AI accelerators and SMIC's manufacturing advances are the leading indicators of how quickly China can build an independent semiconductor ecosystem despite export controls.

The rise of custom silicon: As hyperscalers (Google, Amazon, Microsoft, Meta) invest billions in custom chip design, the question is whether NVIDIA's GPU monopoly erodes or whether the CUDA ecosystem proves too entrenched to dislodge.

Advanced packaging: Chiplet architectures and 3D stacking are becoming as important as process node shrinks. Companies that master advanced packaging may gain advantages independent of transistor density — a potential inflection point for the industry.

The semiconductor industry sits at the intersection of physics, economics, and geopolitics in a way that no other technology sector does. Understanding it is not just an exercise in tech analysis — it is essential context for understanding where AI, computing, and global power are headed.
