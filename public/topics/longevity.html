Longevity & Life Extension:
The Science of Living Longer and Better

David Kingsley, PhD
Scientist · Neural NeXus
March 2026 · 12 min read

In 2022, Saudi Arabia announced the Hevolution Foundation with a planned budget of $1 billion per year for aging research. In 2023, Jeff Bezos-backed Altos Labs launched with $3 billion in funding and a mission to reverse cellular aging. Calico, Alphabet's longevity subsidiary, has been quietly operating since 2013 with over $1.5 billion deployed. The total investment in longevity science now exceeds $4 billion, and that number is accelerating.

Something has changed. Aging research has moved from the margins of biology to the center of biomedical investment. The shift is not driven by hype alone — it is driven by a series of genuine scientific breakthroughs over the past two decades that have transformed aging from an inevitable, intractable process into something that looks increasingly like a targetable biological mechanism. The question is no longer whether aging can be slowed. It is how much, how soon, and for whom.

As a scientist, I find this one of the most consequential areas in all of biology right now. Here is a clear-eyed look at where the science actually stands.

The Hallmarks of Aging

The modern scientific framework for understanding aging was crystallized in a landmark 2013 paper by Carlos Lopez-Otin and colleagues, which proposed nine hallmarks of aging — fundamental biological processes that drive the deterioration we associate with growing old. An updated version in 2023 expanded this to twelve hallmarks. These hallmarks are not a list of symptoms; they are a list of causes, each supported by extensive experimental evidence.

The twelve hallmarks are: genomic instability (accumulation of DNA damage over time), telomere attrition (shortening of protective chromosome caps), epigenetic alterations (changes in gene regulation without DNA sequence changes), loss of proteostasis (failure to maintain properly folded proteins), disabled macroautophagy (decline in cellular recycling), deregulated nutrient sensing (dysfunction in pathways like mTOR and insulin/IGF-1), mitochondrial dysfunction (decline in cellular energy production), cellular senescence (accumulation of damaged, non-dividing cells that secrete inflammatory signals), stem cell exhaustion (loss of regenerative capacity), altered intercellular communication (chronic low-grade inflammation, or "inflammaging"), chronic inflammation, and dysbiosis (disruption of the gut microbiome).

What makes this framework powerful is that each hallmark represents a potential intervention point. Target the hallmark, and you may be able to slow, halt, or even reverse that component of aging. This is not speculation — it has been demonstrated in animal models for several of these hallmarks already.

Key Interventions Under Investigation

Senolytics: Clearing Zombie Cells

Senescent cells are cells that have stopped dividing but refuse to die. Instead, they accumulate in tissues and secrete a toxic cocktail of inflammatory signals called the SASP (senescence-associated secretory phenotype). Think of them as "zombie cells" — they are not functioning normally, but they are actively damaging the tissue around them. Senolytics are drugs designed to selectively kill these cells. The most studied combination is dasatinib + quercetin (D+Q), which has shown remarkable results in mice: clearing senescent cells extends healthspan, improves physical function, and in some models extends lifespan by 20-30%. Human clinical trials are underway for conditions including idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis, diabetic kidney disease, and Alzheimer's disease.

Rapamycin and mTOR Inhibition

Rapamycin, originally discovered as an antifungal compound from Easter Island soil bacteria, is the most consistently life-extending drug ever tested in mammals. It works by inhibiting mTOR (mechanistic target of rapamycin), a nutrient-sensing pathway that regulates cell growth and metabolism. When mTOR is dialed down, cells shift from growth mode to maintenance and repair mode. In mice, rapamycin extends lifespan by 10-25% even when started late in life. The catch: rapamycin is an immunosuppressant at high doses, used clinically to prevent organ transplant rejection. The longevity community is exploring low-dose, intermittent protocols that may capture the anti-aging benefits while minimizing immune suppression. Multiple clinical trials are testing rapamycin analogs for age-related conditions.

NAD+ Precursors

NAD+ (nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide) is a critical coenzyme present in every cell, essential for energy metabolism, DNA repair, and sirtuin activation. NAD+ levels decline dramatically with age — by age 60, levels may be half of what they were at 20. Precursors like NMN (nicotinamide mononucleotide) and NR (nicotinamide riboside) can boost NAD+ levels when taken orally. In mice, NAD+ supplementation improves mitochondrial function, enhances DNA repair, and reverses several markers of aging. Human evidence is more mixed — clinical trials show NAD+ levels do increase, but the downstream effects on healthspan and function are still being established. David Sinclair's work at Harvard has been central to this field, though not without controversy.

Caloric Restriction and Fasting Mimetics

Caloric restriction (CR) — reducing calorie intake by 20-40% without malnutrition — is the oldest and most robustly demonstrated intervention for extending lifespan in laboratory animals, from yeast to primates. The CALERIE trial, the first controlled study of CR in healthy humans, showed that even modest caloric restriction (about 12%) over two years improved multiple biomarkers of aging and reduced estimated biological age. But sustained CR is difficult for most humans. This has driven interest in fasting mimetics — drugs that trick the body into a fasting-like state. Metformin, the widely used diabetes drug, is the subject of the TAME (Targeting Aging with Metformin) trial, the first FDA-approved clinical trial that treats aging itself as the indication.

Epigenetic Reprogramming: The Yamanaka Factor Approach

Perhaps the most radical approach to longevity comes from cellular reprogramming. In 2006, Shinya Yamanaka showed that four transcription factors (Oct4, Sox2, Klf4, c-Myc — the "Yamanaka factors") could reprogram adult cells back into stem cells. The longevity insight: partial reprogramming — brief, controlled exposure to these factors — can reset a cell's epigenetic age without fully reverting it to a stem cell state. In mice, partial reprogramming has been shown to restore youthful gene expression patterns, improve tissue function, and extend lifespan in progeroid (accelerated aging) models. This is the core technology behind Altos Labs' $3 billion bet. The challenge is enormous — too much reprogramming causes cancer (teratomas). But if the dose and timing can be controlled, this approach could represent the closest thing to true biological age reversal.

The paradigm shift: Longevity research has shifted from fringe to frontier science. The question is no longer whether aging is a biological process that can be modulated — the evidence is overwhelming that it can. The question is whether we can translate interventions that work in mice into safe, effective therapies for humans, and whether regulatory frameworks will evolve fast enough to allow it.

Healthspan vs. Lifespan: The Critical Distinction

The longevity field has increasingly shifted its focus from lifespan (how long you live) to healthspan (how long you live in good health). This distinction matters enormously. Global life expectancy has risen dramatically over the past century — from roughly 47 years in 1900 to over 73 today — but much of that gain has come from reductions in infant mortality and infectious disease, not from slowing aging itself. The result: many people live longer but spend their final decade or more in a state of chronic disease, disability, and declining function.

The promise of longevity science is not merely adding years to life, but compressing morbidity — shortening the period of decline at the end of life. If a 75-year-old could have the cardiovascular health, cognitive function, and physical resilience of a 55-year-old, that is arguably more valuable than simply extending lifespan by five years while maintaining the same trajectory of decline. Many researchers now argue that healthspan, not lifespan, should be the primary endpoint for longevity interventions.

This reframing also matters for regulation. The FDA does not currently recognize aging as a disease, which makes it difficult to run clinical trials with "slowing aging" as a primary endpoint. The TAME trial's innovation is that it targets multimorbidity — the co-occurrence of age-related diseases — rather than aging per se, creating a potential regulatory pathway that could unlock future aging-focused drug development.

What to Watch

The next three to five years will be decisive for the field. Several developments deserve close attention. The TAME trial (Targeting Aging with Metformin), if successful, will establish the first FDA-recognized pathway for treating aging as a condition. Results from Loyal's dog longevity drug could provide the first real-world evidence that pharmaceutical lifespan extension works in a complex mammal outside a laboratory. Altos Labs' first clinical candidates, expected to emerge from their reprogramming platform by 2027-2028, will test whether partial cellular rejuvenation is safe enough for human use. And the ongoing Dog Aging Project, a longitudinal study of rapamycin in companion dogs, will generate invaluable data on mTOR inhibition in a species that shares our environment and many of our diseases.

The regulatory landscape is also evolving. The WHO added an extension code for aging (XT9T) to the International Classification of Diseases in 2022. Several countries, including Singapore and the UAE, are actively developing regulatory frameworks for longevity therapeutics. If any major jurisdiction creates a formal pathway for approving drugs that target aging, it will unleash a wave of clinical development that dwarfs what we have seen so far.

Longevity science is no longer a collection of speculative ideas and supplement marketing. It is a rigorous, well-funded, rapidly advancing field with real interventions approaching clinical viability. The biology is increasingly clear. The interventions are increasingly specific. The money is flowing. Whether the first true anti-aging drug reaches patients in five years or fifteen, the trajectory is unmistakable: aging is becoming medicine's next great target.
