Artificial Womb Technology:
The Future of Ectogenesis

David Kingsley, PhD
Scientist · Colossal Biosciences
March 2026 · 9 min read

In 2017, a team at Children's Hospital of Philadelphia placed a premature lamb fetus into a fluid-filled polyethylene bag, connected it to an oxygenator via umbilical catheters, and let it develop for four weeks. The lamb grew normally, developed a wool coat, and opened its eyes. It was, functionally, an artificial womb — and it worked.

That experiment, which the researchers called a "Biobag," made global headlines because it demonstrated something that had been considered science fiction for decades: ectogenesis — gestation outside a biological womb — was not just theoretically possible. It was scientifically tractable. The question shifted from whether it could be done to when, and to what extent.

As a scientist working on reproductive technologies, I spend time thinking about these questions not just academically but practically. Artificial womb technology sits at the intersection of neonatal medicine, reproductive biology, bioethics, and social transformation. Few technologies being developed today carry more potential — or more complexity.

What Is Ectogenesis?

Ectogenesis is the gestation of a fetus in an artificial environment outside the biological womb. The term comes from the Greek ektos (outside) and genesis (origin). It exists on a spectrum from partial ectogenesis — supporting premature infants who have already been born — to full ectogenesis, where a fertilized embryo develops to term entirely outside any biological womb.

Modern neonatal intensive care units (NICUs) are already a form of partial ectogenesis. They sustain premature infants born as early as 22–23 weeks of gestation, using incubators, mechanical ventilators, and intravenous nutrition to support development that would otherwise occur inside a womb. The survival rate for infants born at 28 weeks has climbed dramatically over the past decades, from roughly 80% in the 1980s to over 95% today in well-resourced hospitals.

But there's a hard floor. Below about 22–23 weeks, current technology cannot sustain premature infants because their lungs are too underdeveloped to accept air, even with the most sophisticated ventilators. This is where artificial womb technology proposes to bridge the gap — maintaining premature infants in a fluid-filled environment that mimics the womb's conditions until their lungs and other organs mature enough to breathe air.

Current Artificial Womb Research

The Biobag: CHOP's Landmark Experiment

The 2017 Children's Hospital of Philadelphia (CHOP) experiment led by Dr. Alan Flake was the most significant demonstration of artificial womb technology to date. The team placed premature lamb fetuses — equivalent developmentally to human infants at approximately 23–24 weeks — in sterile bags filled with synthetic amniotic fluid. Oxygenation and circulation were maintained by connecting the lambs' umbilical cords to an external oxygenation system, mimicking placental function.

The system was strikingly effective. Lambs that spent four weeks in the Biobag showed normal brain development, normal lung development (a critical marker), normal growth, and normal behavior after transfer to a conventional incubator. The CHOP team has been refining the system and working toward clinical trials for extremely premature human infants.

EVE Therapy: The Australian Approach

A parallel research program at the University of Western Australia developed EVE (Ex-vivo Uterine Environment) Therapy. Where CHOP's Biobag focused on creating a closed, womb-like environment, EVE therapy maintains premature fetuses in a sealed bag of synthetic amniotic fluid connected to a placentally-inspired artificial lung.

The UWA team published results in 2019 showing that lamb fetuses at 95–100 days gestation (equivalent to approximately 24 weeks in humans) could be maintained successfully for up to a week. They've been working through the engineering and biological challenges of extending that duration and improving stability.

Hasuda's BioBag System (Japan)

Japanese researchers at Jichi Medical University have been working on artificial womb technology since the 1990s. Dr. Yoshinori Kuwabara's team developed an early precursor system in 1997 that maintained goat fetuses for weeks, and more recent iterations by Dr. Nobuya Unno have refined the oxygenation and circulatory support systems. Japan's aging demographic and declining birth rate have made reproductive technology a national research priority.

Toward Full Ectogenesis: The Harder Problem

Supporting an already-gestating fetus is a fundamentally different — and far more achievable — challenge than full ectogenesis. Growing an embryo from fertilization to term outside any biological womb requires solving problems that partial ectogenesis systems don't address: implantation biology, early placental development, the complex hormonal dialogue between the embryo and its environment during the first trimester, and the precise developmental cues that guide organogenesis.

These are not near-term problems. Current research — including work using embryo-like structures called "embryoids" or "blastoids" made from stem cells — is beginning to illuminate the early stages of development in ways that weren't possible before. But a system capable of full ectogenesis, even at the research level, is likely decades away.

Key Milestones in Artificial Womb Research

1997

First Sustained Artificial Uterus (Goat)

Kuwabara et al. at Jichi Medical University maintain goat fetuses in an artificial uterine environment for up to 3 weeks — the first sustained demonstration of the concept.

2017

CHOP Biobag — Landmark Lamb Experiment

The Children's Hospital of Philadelphia team demonstrates that extremely premature lamb fetuses can develop normally for four weeks in a sealed, fluid-filled Biobag with umbilical oxygenation.

2019

EVE Therapy — Australian Parallel Program

University of Western Australia publishes EVE (Ex-vivo Uterine Environment) therapy results, showing a week-long successful maintenance of premature lamb fetuses.

2022

Synthetic Mouse Embryos in Artificial Womb

Weizmann Institute researchers grow synthetic mouse embryos (made entirely from stem cells, no egg or sperm) outside the womb for 8.5 days — more than a third of full mouse gestation. A conceptual breakthrough for full ectogenesis research.

2023–2025

Toward Human Clinical Trials (Partial)

CHOP and UWA teams continue refining their systems toward regulatory approval for clinical trials with extremely premature human infants. Regulatory pathway in the US involves FDA Breakthrough Device designation.

2028–2032

Projected: First Human Clinical Trials

If development proceeds on current timelines, clinical trials of partial ectogenesis (artificial womb support for infants at 22–24 weeks) could begin in the late 2020s or early 2030s.

Why Artificial Womb Technology Matters for Medicine

The immediate clinical application is unambiguous and largely uncontroversial: saving extremely premature infants. Every year, roughly 15 million babies are born prematurely worldwide. About 1 million die from complications of preterm birth. Among the youngest survivors, rates of chronic lung disease, brain injury, and developmental disability are significant.

An artificial womb that could safely support infants at 22 weeks — the current threshold of viability — and maintain them in a womb-like environment for weeks until their lungs mature would represent a transformative advance in neonatal medicine. It would not just increase survival rates; it might dramatically improve outcomes for infants who currently survive but suffer lasting neurological and pulmonary complications from premature exposure to air.

The numbers: A 22-week premature infant today has survival odds of roughly 10–20% even at the best hospitals. A functional artificial womb could potentially push that toward 70–80%. That's not a marginal improvement — it's a category change.

Potential Applications Beyond Neonatology

Reproductive Autonomy

Full ectogenesis would fundamentally change the calculus of reproductive choice. People who cannot carry pregnancies — due to uterine absence, hysterectomy, medical contraindications, or transition-related factors — could potentially gestate a biological child. The gestational burden of pregnancy, which falls entirely on one partner in every relationship where pregnancy is involved, could in principle be shared differently or removed entirely.

Conservation Biology

Artificial womb technology has direct applications in wildlife conservation and — yes — de-extinction. Gestating critically endangered species without requiring surrogate animals, maintaining embryos of extinct species as they're reconstructed via CRISPR editing, scaling up reproduction of rare animals: these are all potential applications of ectogenesis technology in biology beyond human medicine.

Space Exploration

Long-duration spaceflight or colonization scenarios raise obvious reproductive questions. The radiation environment, microgravity, and mission constraints that make pregnancy difficult or dangerous in space are precisely the conditions where artificial womb technology could matter. It's a long-range application, but not an implausible one.

The Ethical Landscape

No technology with the potential to reshape reproduction is without ethical complexity, and artificial womb technology is among the more challenging cases.

The Viability Threshold and Abortion Rights

Current legal frameworks around abortion in many jurisdictions hinge on the concept of fetal viability — the gestational age at which a fetus can potentially survive outside the womb. If artificial womb technology pushes that threshold dramatically earlier (to 22 or even 20 weeks), it has direct implications for abortion law. This is not hypothetical — legal scholars are already analyzing these implications carefully. The ethical and legal complexity is significant.

Commodification of Reproduction

If artificial wombs become commercial products, reproduction becomes a market. The history of reproductive technology — from IVF clinics to surrogacy agencies — shows that these markets emerge with speed and often outpace the regulatory and ethical frameworks meant to govern them. Who has access, at what cost, and under what terms are questions that will need serious policy attention.

Welfare of the Fetus

The welfare of infants gestated artificially is both a medical and an ethical question. We don't fully understand what a human fetus experiences or requires beyond nutrition and oxygenation. Sound, movement, temperature variation, hormonal input from the gestating parent — the full complexity of the womb environment is not captured by the fluid-filled bags of current research systems. Ensuring the best possible neurodevelopmental outcomes for artificially gestated infants will require extensive research.

Pressure and Coercion

If artificial wombs become normalized, what happens to the social status of people who choose to carry pregnancies themselves? The history of technology is full of cases where a new option slowly becomes a new expectation. Careful social and policy thinking is needed to ensure artificial womb technology expands options rather than creating new forms of pressure.

The Bottom Line

Artificial womb technology is not science fiction. The partial ectogenesis version — artificial support for extremely premature infants — is in late-stage animal research and moving toward clinical trials. The full ectogenesis version is a legitimate long-term research goal with real scientific progress, though it remains far off.

The near-term clinical application is compelling enough to justify aggressive investment: the potential to save tens of thousands of premature infants annually, with better long-term outcomes, is one of the clearest unmet needs in modern medicine. The longer-term applications — in reproductive autonomy, conservation biology, and beyond — raise profound questions that society will need to grapple with as the technology matures.

This is one of the most consequential areas of biomedical research underway right now. Most people haven't heard of it. That will change in this decade.
